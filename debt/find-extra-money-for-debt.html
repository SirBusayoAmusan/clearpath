7 Realistic Ways to Find Extra Money to Pay Off Debt (Even on a Low Income)

Clearpath Team8 min read

You don't need a raise or a side hustle empire to start paying off debt faster. You just need to find a little extra money each month — and it's probably hiding closer than you think. Let me show you where to look.

Here's the thing nobody tells you when you're trying to get out of debt: the plan is the easy part. You already know you're supposed to throw extra money at your smallest debt and roll it forward. But then you look at your bank account and think, "What extra money? There's nothing left."

I get it. When you're living paycheck to paycheck, "just pay more" feels like a joke. So let's not do that. Instead, let's go hunting for money you already have but aren't using — plus a few gentle ways to bring in a little more. None of these require a second job or selling your car. They're the small, boring, realistic moves that actually add up.

And here's the magic part: even an extra $50 a month thrown at your debt can shave months, sometimes years, off how long you're stuck paying it. Small numbers matter more than you'd think. Let's find yours.

1. Cancel the subscriptions you forgot you're paying for

Let me guess. You have a streaming service you haven't opened in months. Maybe two. An app that charged you $9.99 before you remembered it existed. A gym membership from a New Year's resolution that didn't survive February.

You're not alone — most of us are quietly leaking money to subscriptions we don't use. Sit down tonight, open your bank statement, and read every single charge from the last month. Every one. I promise you'll find at least one "wait, I'm still paying for that?" moment. Cancel it. That's your first $10, $20, maybe $50 a month, found in fifteen minutes. It was your money the whole time — you were just handing it to someone else.

2. Sell the stuff sitting in your closet

Look around your home right now. The bike nobody rides. Clothes with the tags still on. That kitchen gadget you used once. Old phones in a drawer. To you, it's clutter. To someone on Facebook Marketplace or eBay, it's exactly what they've been searching for.

You don't have to do a big garage sale. Just pick five things this weekend, snap a photo, and list them. One person's "meh" is another person's "finally!" A friend of mine cleared $300 in a single weekend just by photographing things she was going to donate anyway. That's $300 straight onto a credit card balance — and a tidier house as a bonus.

3. Call your credit card company and ask for a lower rate

This one feels scary, and it takes about ten minutes, and it works more often than you'd believe. Pick up the phone, call the number on the back of your credit card, and simply ask: "Can you lower my interest rate?"

That's it. You're a paying customer, and they don't want to lose you. Sometimes they say no. But often — especially if you've been paying on time — they'll drop your rate, which means more of your payment goes to the actual debt instead of the interest. You didn't find cash exactly, but you just made every future payment work harder. Ten minutes, potentially hundreds of dollars saved. Worth the awkward phone call.

4. Try a "no-spend" week

Not forever. Just one week. For seven days, you spend money only on the true essentials — food you already planned, gas, bills. No takeout, no "treat yourself," no impulse Amazon cart. It's a little uncomfortable, like a short fast, but here's what happens: at the end of the week you look up and realize you have money left that would normally be gone.

Take that leftover money and send it straight to your debt before you can spend it. Do a no-spend week once a month and you've got a repeatable little money-finding machine. Bonus: it shows you exactly where your money was quietly disappearing to.

5. Switch to a cash-only "fun money" budget

Cards make spending invisible. You tap, and the money vanishes without you feeling it. Cash is different — when you physically hand over a twenty and get a few coins back, your brain actually notices.

Try this: decide what you'll spend on non-essentials this week — eating out, coffee, little treats — take that amount out in cash, and put it in an envelope. When the envelope's empty, you're done until next week. Most people spend noticeably less this way without feeling deprived, and whatever's left in the envelope on payday goes onto the debt. It's an old trick because it works.

6. Put every "found" dollar toward debt

Life occasionally hands you money you weren't counting on — a tax refund, a birthday check from grandma, a work bonus, a rebate, cash back rewards, even the change jar on your dresser. The natural instinct is to treat it as "fun money." And hey, sometimes you should.

But if you're serious about getting out of debt, make a rule: found money goes to debt. You weren't relying on it, so you won't miss it, and a $500 tax refund dropped onto a credit card can leap you forward months in one move. It's the least painful money you'll ever pay, because it never felt like "yours" in the first place.

7. Pick up a little extra income — the easy way

I saved this for last because I know "make more money" is the advice everyone gives and nobody wants to hear when they're already exhausted. So let's keep it realistic. You're not trying to build a business. You just want an extra $50 or $100 here and there.

Think about what you already have or already do. Have a spare room? A weekend of pet-sitting through an app? Selling a skill you already have — tutoring, a few hours of freelance work, helping someone move? Even picking up occasional gig-app work when you have a free evening. The goal isn't to grind yourself into the ground. It's to catch a little extra now and then, and send all of it straight to your debt. Temporary effort, permanent progress.

You don't need to find $1,000. You need to find $50, seven different ways. That's how real people get out of debt.

Now do the most important part

Reading this list won't pay off a single dollar. Doing one thing will. So don't try all seven today — that's a recipe for doing nothing. Just pick the easiest one right now. Open your bank statement and cancel one subscription. Or list one thing for sale. Then take whatever you free up and put it toward your smallest debt.

That's the whole secret. Find a little, send it to your debt, repeat. Do it consistently and that "impossible" balance starts shrinking faster than you thought possible. You've got this — one small win at a time.
